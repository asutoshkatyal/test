Philosophy 1
|
 Introduction to Philosophy
|
 IV Theatre (MWF 10:00am-10:50am) + Discussion
This course is designed to be an introduction to philosophy. Participants will be introduced the nature of philosophical inquiry by considering some
of the most fundamental philosophical issues in the fields of metaphysics, epistemology and ethics. Some of the issues that will be considered include the question of God's existence, the relationship between mind and body, personal identity, free will, our knowledge of the world (and whether we have any), the nature of morality and moral responsibility, etc. The material covered in the course will include selections from both current and historically important philosophers. Course requirements include a mid-term exam, one short paper and a final exam. There are no prerequisites required for the course.

Philosophy 3
|
 Critical Thinking
|
 BUCHN 1940 (MWF 9:00am-9:50am) + Discussion
This is an introductory course in logic and critical thinking. We are concerned with the structure of argumentation and some general methods for distinguishing good reasoning from bad reasoning. After developing the basic concepts, we consider different systems for representing arguments: categorical logic, propositional logic, and (time permitting) probabilistic logic. There will be homework, a midterm, and a final.

Philosophy 4
|
 Introduction to Ethics
|
 BUCHN 1910 (MWF 11:00am-11:50am) + Discussion
This course is an introduction to the philosophical investigation of morality. Among the questions we will consider: What makes certain actions morally impermissible? Do our ends always justify our means? Is there a systematic theory that can capture our moral intuitions? Do the same moral principles apply to everyone, or are moral judgments relative to a particular society, a particular culture, or even to particular individuals? Can moral judgments ever really be true, or are they just expressions of our feelings? In addition to a general investigation of these questions, we will also look at arguments that address particular moral issues surrounding abortion, war, and torture.

Philosophy 7
|
 Biomedical Ethics
|
 SOUTH HALL 1431 (MWF noon-12:50pm) + Discussion
This class will be a study of some of the more controversial moral dilemmas that arise within the field of medicine in particular, and concern issues of life and death in general. The following are just some of the tough questions that we will philosophically examine in this class: When, if ever, is a woman morally justified in getting an abortion? Is it morally wrong to clone human beings? If so, why? If not, why not? Is a physician permitted to put you to death if that is what you as her terminally ill patient desires? What is so controversial about stem-cell research? Is stem-cell research morally permissible? Is it okay to medically experiment on animals? Does every person have a right to receive health care? How truthful does your doctor have to be with you?

Philosophy 100C
|
 Philosophy of Language
|
 BROIDA 1640 (MW 4:00pm-5:15pm) + Discussion
Every day, you do something remarkable: you open your mouth and communicate information to other people. How do we succeed in doing this? How, through emitting a stream of inherently meaningless acoustic signals, do we succeed in conveying thoughts to one another about topics as diverse as current politics, the distant past, and theoretical physics? This course will examine such questions. To do so, we will study the central concepts surrounding how we use language: meaning, reference, and truth. Readings by Frege, Russell, Grice, Strawson, Donnellan, Kripke, Tarski, Davidson, and others.

Philosophy 124C/224C
|
 Philosophy of Space and Time
|
 PHELPS 3515 (TR 12:30pm-1:45pm)
From the time of the Greeks right up to the present day, philosophers and physicists have been thinking and theorizing about Time and Space. Augustine said "What then is time? If no one asks me, I know, if I want to explain it to someone who asks, I do not know." This is pretty much the state today, many centuries later. In this class, we will consider the philosophical treatments of these issues, and those arising from physics, both modern and contemporary, from Kepler and Galileo to Einstein and quantum gravity.

Required Text(s)
Dainton, Time and Space (McGill-Queens University Press, 2001) ISBN-13: 9780773523067

Philosophy 134
|
 Moral Psychology
|
 GIRVETZ 2108 (TR 9:30am-10:45am)
Moral Psychology is a field of study in both philosophy and psychology in which people study phenomena that are both psychological and ethical in nature — such as altruism and egoism, moral motivation, freedom of the will, practical deliberation, praise and blame, moral responsibility, intentional action, and so on. To properly understand such things, the moral psychologist must answer a number of particularly difficult questions about the nature of our actions and the way we do and should evaluate them. Given the interdisciplinary nature of the field of moral psychology, we will look to both empirical and non-empirical work that addresses the topics of the course. If time permits, we may also discuss some work from so-called "experimental philosophy."

Required Text(s)
Reader from UCSB Bookstore

May
Philosophy 138
|
 Normative Ethics
|
 GIRVETZ 1116 (TR 11:00am-12:15pm)
This course will address the fundamental debate in normative ethics between deontologists and consequentialists. We will pay particular attention to the following questions. What is the moral significance of the distinction between killing and letting die? What is the moral significance of the distinction between intended and forseen harm? When, and why, is it permissible to save the greater number? Is it permissible to kill in self-defence? We will study these questions through the work of some of the leading moral philosophers of the last fifty years, including Phillipa Foot, Derek Parfit, T.M. Scanlon, Frances Kamm, Thomas Nagel, Jonathan Bennett, Warren Quinn, and Judith Thompson.

Philosophy 164/264G
|
 Berkeley
|
 GIRVETZ 1115 (TR 11:00am-12:15pm)
This course will examine the philosophy of George Berkeley (1685-1753). We will focus on his immaterialist metaphysics, including his rejection of a mind-independent material reality, his alternative account of the spatial realm as a logical construct from perceptions, and his account of the nature of the thinking self. We will also be looking at his views on meaning and language, and at how these play out in the context of his philosophical theology.
We will focus primarily on Berkeley's major philosophical works, A Treatise Concerning the Principles of Human Knowledge (1710), Three Dialogues between Hylas and Philonous (1713), and Alciphron (1732), aiming first to understand his theories, second to appreciate their strengths and track down their weaknesses, and third to examine how some of his ideas played out (and continue to play out) in later philosophical epochs. Note: It is recommended (though not required) that students take Phil 160 Descartes or (better yet) Phil 163 Locke before approaching Berkeley.

Required Text(s)

George Berkeley, A Treatise Concerning the Principles of Human Knowledge, ed. Jonathan Dancy (Oxford University Press, 1998), ISBN 9780198751618
George Berkeley, Three Dialogues Between Hylas and Philonous, ed. Jonathan Dancy (Oxford University Press, 1998) ISBN 9780198751496
Instructor:
Holden

Philosophy 165/265G
|
 Hume
|
 BLDG 387, RM 103 (TR 2:00pm-3:15pm)
This course will examine the philosophy of David Hume (1711-76), addressing his epistemology, metaphysics, ethics, and philosophy of religion. We will focus primarily on Hume's later philosophical works, the Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding, the Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals and the Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion. We will aim first to understand his theories, second to track down their weaknesses, and third to examine how some of his ideas played out (and continue to play out) in later philosophical epochs.

 Frege

Philosophy 184/284G
|
 Intermediate Modern Logic
|
 BSIF 1217 (MWF 9:00am-9:50am)

We will continue to develop the logical techniques to which the student was introduced in PHIL 183/283G.  These will include:  multiple quantifiers, methods of proof for quantifiers, numerical quantification and related topics in FOL (first-order logic).  In the second half of the course we will turn to the “metatheory” of logic – studying logic itself from a “meta” or “higher” point of view.  We will learn some very elementary set theory, study proofs by mathematical induction, and prove soundness and completeness for FOL.  If time permits, we will glimpse a bit beyond to sketch the ideas of Gödel’s Incompleteness Theorems. [We hope to cover much of Chapters 11-19 of the text].
Grades will be based on attendance = 1/7, exercises = 1/7, a midterm examination = 2/7, and a final examination = 3/7.

Jon Barwise & John Etchemendy, et al., Language, Proof and Logic (CSLI Publications, Stanford, 2007) ISBN 1-57586-374-X
Instructor:
Anderson
