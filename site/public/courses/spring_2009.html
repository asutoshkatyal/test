Philosophy 1
|
 Introduction to Philosophy
| NH 1006
 (MWF 9:00am-9:50am) + Discussion

In this course, students will be introduced to the discipline of philosophy (of the western, analytic, academic sort). The introduction will be to both the characteristic methods and works in the discipline. We will do this by understanding (and, to some extent, evaluating) work in some of the major fields of philosophy: metaphysics, epistemology, and ethics. Some of the issues we'll likely consider include: the question of God's existence, the relationship between mind and body, personal identity, free will, our knowledge of the world (and whether we have any), the nature of morality and moral responsibility, and so on. The material covered in the course will include selections from both current and historically important philosophers.

Required Text(s)

Timmons and Shoemaker, Knowledge, Nature, and Norms: An Introduction to Philosophy, 1st ed. (Wadsworth, 2008) ISBN-13: 9780495097228

|
 Critical Thinking
|
 HFH 1104 (MWF 10:00am-10:50am) + Discussion
This is an introductory course in logic and critical thinking. We are concerned with the structure of argumentation and some general methods for distinguishing good reasoning from bad reasoning. Philosophy 4
|
 Introduction to Ethics
|
 LSB 1001 (MWF Noon -12:50pm) + Discussion
The types of questions that we will consider in this course can be divided into three classes: 1) Metaethics is concerned with foundational questions in ethics. This includes questions about the nature of ethical truths (if there even are any ethical truths), what we mean when we use ethical language, how we come to know ethical truths (if we even can), and how we are motivated by ethical truths (and what this might reveal about the nature of ethical truths); 2) Normative ethics is concerned with what it is that makes something good/bad, right/wrong, etc. In particular, we will study the ethical theories of J.S. Mill, Immanuel Kant, and Thomas Hobbes; 3) Applied ethics is concerned with answering particular ethical questions such as: When, if ever, is abortion permissible? When, if ever, is terrorism/torture justified? Do we have a duty to end suffering due to hunger in other countries? etc.

Required Text(s)
Mill, Utilitarianism (Oxford Philosophical Texts Paperback) ISBN-13: 9780198751632
Kant, Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals (Cambridge) ISBN-13: 9780521566735

Philosophy 20C
|
 History of Philosophy
|
 BRDA 1610 (MWF 11:00am-11:50am) + Discussion
In this this course, we examine the main metaphysical and epistemological systems in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries: the so-called early modern period. Specifically, we will examine the work of four major figures in the period: John Locke (1632-1704), George Berkeley (1685-1753), David Hume (1711-1776) and Immanuel Kant (1724-1804). Although the focus of the course will be the metaphysical and epistemological views, we may dabble in Hume's ethical view.

Philosophy 130/230G
|
 Freedom and Determinism
| NH 1105
(TR 11:00am-12:15pm)
Determinism is the doctrine that the laws of nature plus the past necessitate the future: given the laws and the past, only one future is possible. Is determinism compatible with the view that we often act freely? Is determinism compatible with the view that we often are morally responsible for what we do? Is determinism true? Are we free and responsible?

Van Inwagen, An Essay On Free Will, 1986 edition (Oxford) ISBN-10: 0198249241

Philosophy 139
|
 Meta-Ethics
|
 GIRV 1116 (TR 12:30am-1:45pm)

We will discuss moral realism, the view that there are moral truths which sounds great until you try to explain it as well as some forms of moral antirealism. The thread running through these views is naturalism, the attempt to fit morality into the picture of the world we get from the natural sciences. Most of the popular realist and antirealist theories are naturalistic in some sense; so while evaluating how well they live up to the ethical data, we can also evaluate whether they meet their naturalistic goals. We will also assess the content and plausibility of naturalism, in light of difficulties reconciling it with what we know about morality.

The recommended prerequisites for this course are an ethics course (e.g., Phil 4 or 100A)
and a non-ethics course from the 100 series (100B, 100F).

Required Text(s)
Reader, available at Grafikart in Isla Vista
Instructor:
Manning

Philosophy 149/249G
|
 Action Theory
|
 ARTS 1426 (TR 9:30am-10:45am)
Humans (and other animals) are not wholly passive with respect to our environment; we sometimes act on the world in order to achieve our ends. Humans, unlike most other animals, can reason critically about which ends are worth achieving and about how best to achieve them. Philosophy of action investigates our capacities as agents and as rational agents. In this course we will explore metaphysical questions about the causation, explanation, and individuation of intentional actions, epistemological questions about the structure of practical reasoning and our knowledge of our own actions, and meta-ethical questions about the connection between action and value.

Required Text(s)
Anscombe, Intention, 2nd ed. (Harvard) ISBN-13: 9780674003996
Instructor:
Nance

Philosophy 151
|
 Pre-Socratics
|
 ARTS 1426 (TR 12:30pm-1:45pm)

Greek philosophy had been flourishing for over a century when Socrates was born (469 BCE). Socrates’ thought, as well as that of all later Greek philosophy, was strongly influenced by the considerations of these “pre-Socratic” philosophers. The aim of this course is to examine the theories, arguments and concepts of these thinkers and to show why they are important both historically as well as in their own right. The requirements for the course include a take mid-term exam and a take-home final exam. There are no prerequisites required for the course.

Required Text(s)
Warren, Presocratics: Natural Philosophers Before Socrates (U. of California) ISBN-13: 9780520253698
 
Instructor:
Tennberg

Philosophy 297A
|
 Seminar in the History of Philosophy
|
 SH 5617 (T 3:30pm - 6:20pm)

This seminar will focus on the epistemology and metaphysics of David Hume (1711-76). We will also cover aspects of his philosophy of mind, metaethics, and philosophy of religion insofar as these fall under ‘metaphysics and epistemology’ broadly construed. There won’t be much attention to his practical philosophy such as his first-order ethics, political philosophy, and economics, however.

We will focus primarily on Hume’s major works in M&E: the Treatise, the Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding, and the Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion.
