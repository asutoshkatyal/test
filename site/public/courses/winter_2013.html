Philosophy 1
|
 Introduction to Philosophy
|
 EMBAR HALL | (WF 2:00pm-3:15pm) + Discussion
An introduction to the problems and methods of contemporary philosophy, focusing upon metaphysics and epistemology. We will discuss such questions as: Is there life after death? Does God exist? Do I have a soul distinct from my body? What is the nature of consciousness? Could other forms of intelligent life exist, or is cognition the unique endowment of human beings? Could the external world be radically different than I perceive it to be? Readings drawn from both historical and contemporary sources. We will also discuss philosophically relevant items from popular culture, such as the movies "The Matrix," "The Truman Show," "Blade Runner," and others.

Philosophy 20B
|
 History of Philosophy: Medievals to Rationalism
|
 GIRV 1004 | (MWF 2:00pm-2:50pm) + Discussion

This course will introduce students to some of the main philosophers and arguments from the medieval period to the early modern period (roughly, 400-1650CE).  We will primarily focus on four key figures throughout this era:  Augustine, Anselm, Thomas Aquinas, and Rene Descartes.  We will examine some of their philosophical arguments and positions with respect to knowledge, the existence of God, human nature, and free will.  We will also cover two key issues during the medieval period:  the problem of universals and competing moral theories (e.g., natural law & divine command).

Philosophy 141
|
 History of Ethics
|
 GIRV 2119| (MW 3:30pm-4:45pm)

This course is an examination of contemporary virtue ethics.  Although virtue ethics is an ethical theory with ancient roots, it is nevertheless a developing system of thought. In contrast to most ethical systems, which focus on the rightness or wrongness of actions, virtue ethics emphasizes the goodness or badness of those who act.  Virtue theory is concerned with the development of good character, which is understood by reference to the virtues (justice, courage, generosity, self-control, etc.).  For virtue theorists, good character bears an especially close relation to human flourishing, happiness, and the ability to construct a meaningful human life.

Philosophy 150B/250B
|
 Adv. Theory of Knowledge
|
 SH 5617 | (WF 12:30pm-1:45pm)

We will study one of the oldest arguments in philosophy: the regress argument. This argument tightly constrains possible theories regarding the structure of justification. We will explore the principal options, with an emphasis on foundationalism, coherentism, and infinitism. Our main goal is to investigate the thesis that there is "immediate justification," in a sense that we will make precise. We will discuss examples drawn from perception, memory, and testimony. Readings by Stroud, Wright, Burge, Peacocke, Alston, Audi, Pryor, Klein, and others.

Philosophy 176/276G
|
 Historical Philosophers
|
 GIRV 2129| (TR 2:00pm-3:15pm)

This course will examine the philosophical system of Nagarjuna (c.150-250 AD), the founder of the Madhyamaka ('Middle Path') school of Mahayana Buddhism, and one of the great figures of classical Indian philosophy. The main focus will be Nagarjuna's metaphysics, including his accounts of causation, change and motion, the status of self, and the 'emptiness' (or lack of ultimate, convention-independent reality) of all phenomena. There will also be some attention to his philosophy of language and to his relativistic and anti-foundationalist epistemology.

Please note that there will be little attention to the spiritual or soteriological aspects of Nagarjuna's philosophy, and students who enroll in this class in the hope of finding some deep or exotic Eastern spiritual wisdom on offer will be sorely disappointed. Rather, this will be a course focused exclusively on quite technical issues of logic, metaphysics, epistemology and language

Philosophy 186/286G
|
 Philosophical Logic
|
 GIRV 2119| (TR 2:00pm-3:15pm)

Our topic will be modal logic: that subfield of formal logic dealing with the validity of arguments as it depends on the modal notions: necessity, possibility, impossibility, strict implication, contingent truth or falsity, and the like. We will study natural deduction formulations of the main modal logics, "possible worlds" semantics, completeness proofs, and the use of diagrams for evaluating arguments. We will strive to get into the details of quantified modal logic by the end of the term, but experience has shown that we will probably only be able to give a superficial treatment of some of that material. Philosophical applications and problems of interpretation will be occasionally considered. Reading and exercises will be assigned for virtually every class date. The text is: James Garson, Modal Logic for Philosophers, which the student can buy on the internet. Grades will be based on homework (1/6), a midterm exam (2/6), and a final exam (3/6). One or both of the exams will be take-home. There will be an extra 50 minute session each week for 212, time and place to be arranged.

Philosophy 297A
|
 Seminar in History of Philosophy
|
 SH 5617| (T 4:00pm-6:50pm)

This seminar will examine Hobbes's philosophy of religion, as it relates to his metaphysics and epistemology on the one hand, and to his political philosophy on the other. Topics will include Hobbes's attitude to the traditional arguments of natural religion, his expressivist treatment of religious language, his account of the relationship between the divine will and the laws of nature, his treatment of Scripture and the authority of the prophets, his treatment of miracles, his Erastianism regarding public religious practice, and his natural history of religion.

In the background will be the issue of Hobbes's sincerity and strategic purposes when addressing religion. Is he a sincere Christian, trying to reconcile the new science and his materialist metaphysics with some sort of Calvinism? Is he some other sort of theist? Or is he more irreligious -- perhaps some sort of deist, agnostic, or atheist more interested in debunking religion or taming the dangers that religious sectarianism poses to the commonwealth? All these interpretations have their advocates. We'll look into it.
