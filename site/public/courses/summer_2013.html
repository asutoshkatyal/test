Philosophy 145
|
 Punishment and Responsibility
|
 SH 1430 | Session A (TWR 2:00pm-3:25pm)

On July 20, 2012, James Holmes opened fire at a movie theater in Colorado. The shooting left 12 people dead and another 58 injured. According to federal agents, two months prior to the attack, Holmes began amassing an arsenal that includes tear gas, handguns, a shotgun, an AR-15 rifle, and thousands of rounds of ammunition. He also allegedly booby-trapped his apartment with explosives. He is currently charged with murder and attempted murder, and is awaiting trial.

Cases such as this open up a wide range of questions about the nature and justification of punishment. Among them are:

[1]    What is punishment?

[2]    What justifications do we have to punish?

[3]    Given those justifications, what is the appropriate punishment in a given case?

[4]    Who can we punish? Who are exempt from punishment?

In this course, we will explore both classical and contemporary responses that have been given to these questions. We will also enhance our understanding by applying these responses to real-life cases, like that of James Holmes. By the end of the course, students should have an understanding of the different theories on the nature and justification of punishment, their application, and challenges to them. Students should also have a better understanding of their own commitments on these issues. Moreover, through the reading and writing requirements, along with discussion of the content of the course material, my aim is for students to develop their reading, writing, and critical thinking skills.

Philosophy 188
|
Theory of Value | GIRV 2129 | Session B (TWR 11:00am-12:25pm)
We will consider the central philosophical questions about the nature of value. Are values (all) subjective? Are there such things as intrinsic values? Can values be measures, and if so, how? Can we reason logically about values? We will also look at a particular philosophical system which involves a ranking of values.

Required Text(s)
Nicholas Rescher. Introduction to Value Theory, Prentice-Hall 1969 (Out of print, but obtainable online. Cf. addall.com).
