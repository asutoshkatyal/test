Philosophy 1
|
 Introduction to Philosophy
|
 BUCHN 1910 (MWF 10:00-10:50AM) + Discussion
This course will be an introduction to such branches of philosophy as metaphysics and epistemology. We will look at attempts by philosophers to answer such questions as: Does God exist? Do I know anything about a world external to my sensory experiences? Does such knowledge as I may have derive only from experience, or do I have non-experiential knowledge? What am I (a body? a mind? a composite of the two?)? We will read works by Plato, Aquinas, Descartes, Locke, Hume, Kant, Reid, Fodor, Searle, Nagel, and Parfit.

Required Text(s)
Longman, Philosophical Problems: An Annotated Anthology

Philosophy 4
|
 Introduction to Ethics
|
 EMBAR HALL (MWF 11:00-11:50am) + Discussion
Isn’t ethics just whatever you believe? Can morality be objective? What makes something right or wrong? Are good intentions enough? Or good character? What is the difference? How should ethics guide my actions? This course will survey some of the best available answers to these questions. We will examine several ethical philosophies, including those of Aristotle, Thomas Hobbes, David Hume, Immanuel Kant, and John Stuart Mill. Finally, we will use these theories to analyze situations that confront us daily.

Philosophy 112/212G
|
 Philosophy of Religion
|
 NH 1105 (TR 9:30-10:45am)
An introductory survey of a number of philosophical issues connected with the concepts and beliefs making up the great monotheistic religious traditions of Judaism, Christianity and Islam. A large portion of the course deals with the concept of God and belief in the existence of God. We will examine what have been historically the most important arguments for the existence of God—the cosmological argument, the argument from design, and the ontological argument—as well as consider the evidential value for religious belief provided by the alleged occurrence of miracles, by the variety of religious or mystical experiences and by the alleged objectivity of morality. Then we will consider grounds and arguments against the existence of God, beginning with alleged incoherencies in the very concept of God and ending with an extensive consideration of what many consider the biggest obstacle to religious belief—the problem of evil and suffering. Finally, we will stand back from our deliberations and ask: in the light of all the arguments pro and con, is belief in God rational? At each stage we will come to grips with some of the best our ancestors have contributed to these issues—works by Plato, Aquinas, Anselm, Descartes, Hume, Kant, Pascal, Freud, James and Russell—as well as important writings of contemporary philosophers. Requirements are: final exam, plus two short papers.

Required Text(s)
Hume, Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion
Pojman, Philosophy of Religion
Reader
Instructor:
Forgie

Philosophy 134/234G
|
 Moral Psychology
|
 LSB 1101 (TR 12:30-1:45pm)
Moral psychology is an attempt to understand virtue and evil, praise and blame, pride and humility, freedom and responsibility, practical deliberation and action. To explain these phenomena the moral psychologist must answer a number of historically persistent, thorny questions about the nature of action and evaluation. In this course we will concentrate on three different characters 'the altruist, the amoralist, and the addict'and the light they throw on some of these questions. The questions we will ask include: Is altruism possible? If it is not, why can't we act for the sake of others? If it is possible, does this mean we can act on something other than our desires? Are there amoralists? Can someone have an entirely correct set of moral views (judgments or opinions) while having no motivation to act morally? If so, does this show that moral judgments are not intrinsically motivating? What is addiction, and how does it relate to more ordinary forms of temptation and weakness of will? What is it to resist a desire or give into temptation? Is it ever irrational to yield to one's strongest desire? If acting on one's strongest desire can be irrational, when is it, and why? When are people responsible for some moral or immoral action? When is it appropriate to praise or blame someone for what she has done? If its initial state and the laws of physics fully determined the future state of the universe, could we freely decide how to act? If free decisions and actions are compatible with the truth of determinism, when, if ever, is a decision or action not free? We will look for answers to these questions in the work of Michael Bratman, James Drier, Harry Frankfurt, Christine Korsgaard, Elijah Millgram, John Robertson, Peter Strawson, Peter van Inwagen, Gary Watson, David Wiggins, Bernard Williams and perhaps others. Requirements for the course consist in two medium length papers, both of which must be rewritten in response to comments. Additional shorter assignments are possible as well.

Required Text(s)
Reader
Instructor:
Zimmerman

Philosophy 139/239G
|
 Meta-Ethics
|
 HSSB 1214 (TR 11:00am-12:15pm)
We will study the best attempts by philosophers to answer, or throw light on, questions concerning the meaning and justification of ethical claims. For example, we will consider G.E. Moore's analysis of the so-called "Naturalistic Fallacy", in which he argues that no definition of the term "good" is possible at all. And we will examine David Hume's argument to the effect that no ethical conclusion can be validly inferred from purely factual premisses ("Is never implies ought".) We will also consider representative samples of the best proposed attempts to analyze or define ethical terms such as "good", "ought", and "right". (For example, it has been urged that "Honesty is good" means, roughly, "I like honesty! Won't you like it too?". Others have argued that it means "Hoorah for honesty!". Still others have suggested that it is synonymous with an imperative, say, "Be honest!". It has even been claimed that "Honesty is good" means: "Honesty possesses a certain objective, 'non-natural' property: Goodness". Finally, we will take an in-depth look at the elementary part of Deontic Logic, an attempt to codify the basic structure of (some) ethical reasoning.

Required Text(s)
Reader
Instructor:
Anderson

|
 Leibniz
|
 GIRV 1115 (TR 2:00-3:15pm)
We will study the metaphysical system of the great rationalist philosopher Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646-1716). Leibniz is said by many to be the greatest systematic metaphysician who ever lived. Philosophy 296B
|
 Seminar in Epistemology
|
 SH 5617 (T 3:30-6:20pm)
The Epistemology of Normative Facts: We will begin by dismissing normative/evaluative (n/e) expressivism--which we will understand as the claim that n/e vocabulary is never used to assert anything truth-evaluable (i.e. there are no n/e propositions).  We are then left with three meta-normative possibilities: (1) nihilism (we assert propositions about what ought to be done and what is better than what, but every positive (or unnegated) assertion of this sort is false); (2) anti-realism or constructivism (there are n/e facts and they are  constituted by our actual or ideal opinions, emotions or reactive attitudes ); and (3) realism (there
are n/e facts and they are not so constituted).  To rationally reject nihilism in favor of realism or anti-realism is to conclude (come to know or justifiably believe) that there are normative and evaluative facts or truths.  Can we acquire knowledge (or justified belief) of this sort?  If so, how does it differ from our knowledge of (or justified belief in) the existence of non-n/e facts?  If we conclude that we cannot have normative knowledge, how can we go on, knowing (as we would) that we can never know what we should do from one moment to the next?

Required Text(s)
Sinnott-Armstrong, Moral Skepticisms
Reader
Instructor:
Zimmerman

Philosophy 297A
|
 Seminar in History of Philosophy
|
 SH 5617 (R 3:30 - 6:20pm)
This title of this seminar is "Skepticism, fideism and natural religion: Hume and some predecessors".

We will examine some of the central topics in early modern philosophy of religion, mainly through a close study of Hume's comprehensive assault on natural theology in the Dialogues and first Enquiry, but also through the Christian apologetics of George Berkeley, and the writings of earlier figures such as the fideists Michel de Montaigne and Pierre Bayle.

Required Text(s)

Montaigne, Apology for Raymond Sebond
