Philosophy 1
|
 Introduction to Philosophy
|
 (MWF 10:00-10:50AM) + Discussion

This course is an introduction to some of the perennial problems and methods of philosophy, focusing on metaphysics and the theory of knowledge. Some of the issues we will address include the question of God’s existence, the relationship between mind and body, whether we have free will and the nature of moral responsibility, our knowledge of the world (and whether we have any) and, if there is time, some philosophical puzzles and paradoxes.

Philosophy 4
|
 Introduction to Ethics
|
 EMBAR HALL (MWF 11:00am-11:50am) + Discussion
In this course we will analyze some basic moral concepts and ask some fundamental questions about the nature of morality: Is morality objective? Do moral claims like "murder is wrong" express trans-cultural truths, or are they mere matters of taste? If moral claims do express truths, what is it in the world that makes them true? Perhaps its God, or self-interest, or Reason, or maximized happiness, or evolution, or . . . After critically examining these options, we will (if there is time) turn our attention to some practical issues in applied ethics.

Philosophy 100D
|
 Philosophy of Mind
|
 (TR 11:00-12:15pm) + Discussion
Humans can think, doubt, yearn, and regret. Rocks can do none of these things. This is because humans have minds, while rocks do not. But why are we so confident that humans have minds and rocks do not? What about intermediate cases like dolphins, dogs, or trees? Even if I am sure that I have a mind, why should I be so sure that you do? What does it even mean to say that a creature has a mind? Does it mean that the creature has something like an immaterial soul? Does it mean that, unlike a rock, the creature’s behavior is complicated enough to warrant attributing mental activity to it? Does it mean that the creature has a sufficiently complex brain? We will study the various ways in which philosophers have tried to answer these questions during the past century.

Philosophy 112/212G
|
 Philosophy of Religion
|
 (TR 9:30pm-10:45am)
An introductory survey of a number of philosophical issues connected with the concepts and beliefs making up the great monotheistic religious traditions of Judaism, Christianity and Islam. A large portion of the course deals with the concept of God and belief in the existence of God. We will examine what have been historically the most important arguments for the existence of God—the cosmological argument, the argument from design, and the ontological argument—as well as consider the evidential value for religious belief provided by the alleged occurrence of miracles, by the variety of religious or mystical experiences and by the alleged objectivity of morality. Then we will consider grounds and arguments against the existence of God, beginning with alleged incoherencies in the very concept of God and ending with an extensive consideration of what many consider the biggest obstacle to religious belief—the problem of evil and suffering. Finally, we will stand back from our deliberations and ask: in the light of all the arguments pro and con, is belief in God rational? At each stage we will come to grips with some of the best our ancestors have contributed to these issues—works by Plato, Aquinas, Anselm, Descartes, Hume, Kant, Pascal, Freud, James and Russell—as well as important writings of contemporary philosophers. Requirements are: final exam, plus two short papers.

Required Text(s)
Hume, Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion, ISBN-13: 9780140445367
Pojman, Philosophy of Religion, 5th ed., ISBN-13: 9780495095040

Philosophy 124B/224B
|
 Philosophy of Physics
|
 (TR 12:30pm-1:45pm)

In this course, we will discuss advances in physics against the historical and philosophical backgrounds in which they occurred. A major goal is to impress upon the student the essential and ineliminable role the philosophical considerations have played in the actual practice of science and in the construction of scientific theories. Though we will begin with a treatment of the physical science of the ancient and Renaissance eras, most of the course will be devoted to the theories of physics developed in the 20th century.

Philosophy 150D/250D
|
 Adv. Phil. of Mind
|
 (TR 2:00pm-3:15pm)
One of the most striking features of the mind is its capacity to represent. For instance, I have beliefs that represent various facts about Abraham Lincoln, such as that he was president of the United States. What are the facts by virtue of which the mind represents the world? Does mental representation require the existence of mental representations? Should our best theory of the mind cite its representational properties, or is representation a pre-theoretic notion that we should jettison for the purposes of serious inquiry? We will study these perennial questions in light of two pivotal contemporary developments: the thesis that the mind is somehow analogous to a digital computer, and the "externalist" thought experiments pioneered by Hilary Putnam and Tyler Burge.

Required Text(s)
Fodor, Representations: Philosophical Essays, ISBN-13: 9780262560276

Rescorla

Philosophy 150E/250E
|
 Advanced Metaphysics
|
 (MWF 10:00-10:50am)

Course Content:
Our topic will be Causation.  The concept of a cause is central to many philosophical problems and theories.  Does the concept of a cause involve the idea of a necessary connection between events?  Or is a cause just an event that falls under a certain regularity or law of nature.  Perhaps our idea of cause is defective or illusory – a habit of the mind not corresponding to anything in reality, as David Hume argued. Hume also claimed that all our reasonings about “matters of fact” depend on this alleged relation of cause and effect.  He famously concluded from this that we can have no good reason to believe that the unobserved is like the observed.
Since mental events are apparently so very different from physical events, how can a mental event cause a physical event in the brain?  Perhaps events in the brain cause mental events but not vice versa.  Or maybe mental events just are events in the brain and so there is no problem about how one can cause the other. If every event has a cause, our actions are caused and these events have causes and so on into the distant past.  How then can any of our actions be free?  Can it be true, as some philosophers maintain, that there is a special kind of causation, agent causation (as opposed to event causation), that is operative in connection with human choices?  Is the idea of precognition logically or conceptually impossible, involving as it does “backwards causation”?   
These and many other philosophical issues and theories crucially involve the notion of a cause.  We will not be concerned specifically with any of these problems, but rather with the prior problem of understanding the concept of causation itself.  In that regard we will consider the attempts by various philosophers to analyze or clarify the notion (Perhaps we will occasionally take a quick look at the bearing of various proposed analyses of causation on these other problems.)
Grading:
Grades will be based on two essay type examinations: the midterm and the final.

Required Text(s)
Sosa, Causation (Oxford, 1993) ISBN-13: 9780198750949
Reader
Instructor:
Anderson

Philosophy 162/262G
|
 Leibniz
|
 (TR 11:00am-12:15pm)
This course will examine the philosophy of the seventeenth-century rationalist Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646-1716). We will concentrate on understanding his metaphysical system, including his views on truth, necessity and possibility, substance, causation and freedom. We will focus primarily on reading Leibniz’s original texts rather than commentaries. Our aim will be to understand his theories, to appreciate their strengths and track down their weaknesses, and to consider how some of his ideas played out (and ontinue to play out) in later philosophical epochs.

Note: Students should appreciate that Leibniz is one of the more difficult of early modern philosophers. It is recommended (though not required) that students have some background in other early modern philosophers such as Descartes, Spinoza, Locke, or Kant at an upper division level before approaching Leibniz.

Philosophy 163
|
 Locke
|
 (MWF 2:00pm-2:50pm)
This class investigates the views of one of the modern era’s most influential Western thinkers: John Locke. At the heart of Locke’s Philosophy is his departure from two dominant traditions of his time. First, he splits away from the Rationalist canon of Plato and Descartes by arguing that all of our ideas come from sense experience. Secondly, he stands in opposition to absolutists such as Thomas Hobbes by promoting a political system according to which the leaders of government are not afforded absolute power. These are the two ideas that will be the focus of the course.
