Philosophy 4
|
 Introduction to Ethics
|
 BUCHN 1910 (MWF 1:00pm-1:50pm) + Discussion
Are there moral facts we can discover? If so, just how do we go about figuring out what is good, right and just? If not, why does it seem to me as though murder is wrong and kindness a virtue? What should I do if I want to act morally? If I don't want to act morally, is there still a sense in which I should act morally? Should I try to make everyone as happy as possible or should I primarily concern myself with helping myself, or my friends and family? What would a correct moral theory say about such things as abortion, euthanasia and severe global inequality? Is there any way to resolve disputes over whether such things are moral permissible? These are the questions we'll raise and discuss in Philosophy 4.

Three of the most basic metaphysical questions are: (1) "What are the most general features of the World, and what sorts of things does it contain?", (2) "Why does a World exist? and, more specifically, why is there a World having the features described in the answer to Question 1?", and (3) "What is our place in the World? Philosophy 124A/224
|
 Philosophy of Science
|
 SH 5617 (MWF 11:00am-11:50am)
Logical positivism was a philosophical movement that flourished in Vienna during the 1930s. The positivists sought to purge philosophy of what they viewed as its pernicious metaphysical tendencies. They generally dismissed religion and ethics as nonsense. They applauded natural science as a paradigm of knowledge, and they constructed systematic philosophical theories designed to accommodate the latest scientific developments. Collectively, the positivists produced some of the best and most important philosophy of the twentieth century. Even though few people still accept positivism's central doctrines, one cannot even begin to understand contemporary philosophy without examining those doctrines. We will study the major elements of positivism, focusing upon Rudolf Carnap's mature work. We will conclude by discussing W. V. Quine's influential critique of positivism. Note: although the only official prerequisite for this course is one previous philosophy class, prior enrollment in Phil 100F is recommended for undergraduates

Philosophy 150A/250A
|
 Adv. Topics in Ethics
|
 ARTS 1251 (MWF 2:00pm-2:50pm)
Our topic will be deontic logic, the logic of normative concepts such as â€žoughtâ€°, â€žbeing obligatoryâ€°, â€žbeing forbiddenâ€°, and â€žbeing permittedâ€°. Of course we will be mainly interested when these terms are used in their strictly ethical or moral senses, but deontic logic is often developed so as to apply to any of a large class of rules, including, for example, laws or legal rules and the rules of games. Deontic logic was studied by some medieval logicians and by Leibniz but only came to be the subject of intense investigation in modern times when Georg von Wright drew attention to the topic. So far the area has consisted largely of the study of paradoxes or puzzles to which proposed logical systems have given rise, but it has the potential to be a powerful instrument in the philosophical study of ethics and law. We develop the topic from its elementary beginnings to a glimpse of its present boundaries. Prerequisites: A course in introductory logic including the propositional calculus (truth-tables, tautologies, rules for tautological validity, etc.). Some elementary knowledge of quantifier logic will also be useful, although we will give an elementary introduction to the topic when needed.

Philosophy 165
|
 Hume
|
 PHELPS 1508 (MWF 2:00pm-2:50pm)
We will discuss the mature philosophy of the great Empiricist philosopher David Hume (1711-1776). Hume has been profoundly influential in many areas of philosophy, and a good grasp of his views is important for understanding many contemporary debates. Philosophy 183/283
|
 Beginning Modern Logic
|
 SH 1431 (MWF 10:00am-10:50am) + Discussion
This course is an introduction to the study of correct reasoning--drawing inferences, distinguishing good arguments from bad ones, seeing one's way from assumptions to their consequences. Reasoning is a cognitive process or activity employed in mathematics and other disciplines, as well as in everyday decision making, problem solving, argumentation, and discussion. We shall study the principles and techniques of correct reasoning by developing an artificial symbolic system of "natural deduction," which closely mirrors, but sharpens and systematizes, the patterns of ordinary reasoning that we engage in every day.

Required Text(s)
Kalish, Logic: Techniques of Formal Reasoning

Philosophy 187/287G
|
 Philosophy of Mathematics
|
 GIRV 1115 (MW 2:00pm-3:15pm)
An introduction to the three major historical schools of philosophy of mathematics: logicism, intuitionism, and formalism. We will address such questions as: What is the nature of number? For instance, what is the number one? What is the nature of infinity? Is there a notion of mathematical truth distinct from what is provable? Does mathematics describe an objective realm independent of human cognition, or are mathematical entities and facts somehow a product of our own thinking? Given that we discover mathematical truths through a priori reasoning, what explains the astounding applicability of mathematics to the physical universe? Prerequisite: Some familiarity with deductive logic; or a background in mathematics. Open to all mathematics majors and graduate students, and to all computer science majors and graduate students.

Required Text(s)
George, Philosophies of Mathematics
Benacerraf, Philosophy of Mathematics
Instructor:
Rescorla
