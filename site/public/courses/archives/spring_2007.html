Philosophy 1
|
 Introduction to Philosophy
|
 NH 1006 (MWF 9:00-9:50AM) + Discussion
This course will be an introduction to such branches of philosophy as metaphysics and epistemology. We will look at attempts by philosophers to answer such questions as: Does God exist? Do I know anything about a world external to my sensory experiences? Does such knowledge as I may have derive only from experience, or do I have non-experiential knowledge? What am I (a body? a mind? a composite of the two?)? We will read works by Plato, Aquinas, Descartes, Locke, Hume, Kant, Reid, Fodor, Searle, Nagel, and Parfit.

Required Text(s)
Longman, Philosophical Problems: An Annotated Anthology
Instructor:
Hannen

 Teaching Assistant(s): Curtin, Newman

Philosophy 6
|
 Professional and Business Ethics
|
 WEBB 1100 (MWF 1:00-1:50pm) + Discussion
This course will investigate ethical issues that confront those in business, medicine, and the law. Business and professional ethics have recently begun to attract more interest and courses on ethics are required at nearly all business, medical, and law schools. This course has been designed to give students exposure to many of the important issues in these fields and to give them a head start on issues that they will encounter in business and professional schools. The course selections focus mainly on issues that face individual businesspeople, doctors, and lawyers and not on broad subjects that are more institutional in scope (e.g., “can doctors lie to patients?” rather than “is our legal system fair?”). The course will be broken down into the following three units, wherein the following issues are likely to be addressed:

1) Business ethics: Is affirmative action morally permissible? What kinds of contracts, if any, are unconscionable and unenforceable? Is it morally permissible for people to bluff during negotiations? Do companies have obligations other than maximize profits? What is the moral status of sweatshops in third-world countries? Is “whistleblowing” permissible or does it violate fiduciary relationships between employees and employers? What is the moral status of drug testing of employees?

2) Medical ethics: The Hippocratic Oath. What are the moral obligations of the doctor? Is it permissible for doctors to lie to patients? To withhold the truth? What limits, if any, should be placed on patient autonomy? Is there any scope for paternalism within considerations of autonomy? Who determines patient competence? What is the moral status of informed consent? How does the introduction of HMO’s change the obligations of the doctor?

3) Ethics of lawyering: What are the obligations of the lawyer? Which takes priority, considerations of justice or the importance of “zealous advocacy”? Is it morally permissible for a defense lawyer to put witnesses on the stand who he expects to commit perjury? Can he encourage perjurious testimony? Should the “last lawyer in town” be forced to represent a client in need?

Students will be evaluated primarily based on a mid-term exam, final exam, and final paper. Section attendance and participation will also be considered. The course will use a reader which will be available at GrafikArt in Isla Vista.

Required Text(s)
Reader
Instructor:
Huizenga

 Teaching Assistant(s): Blair

Philosophy 20C
|
 History of Philosophy (Empiricists to Kant)
|
 BRDA 1610 (MWF 11:00-11:50am) + Discussion
This is the third and final course in the PHIL 20 series of classes surveying the history of philosophy in the West. In this last course, we examine the main developments in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries: the so-called early modern period. (Note that you do not have to have taken 20A or 20B in order to enroll for 20C.) We will examine the work of four major figures in the period: John Locke (1632-1704), George Berkeley (1685-1753), David Hume (1711-1776) and Immanuel Kant (1724-1804). The focus of the course will be the epistemological and metaphysical systems developed by these pivotal thinkers, and in particular their various theories of the relationship between mind and world. Reading will consist largely in representative selections from each of these four philosophers.

Required Text(s)
Ariew & Watkins, Readings in Modern Philosophy, Volume II
Kant, Prolegomena to Any Future Metaphysics
Instructor:
Holden
|
 Teaching Assistant(s): Altschul, Lee, Lewis, McDonald, Tennberg

Philosophy 130/230G
|
 Freedom and Determinism
|
 GIRV 1112 (TR 11:00am-12:15pm)
Determinism is the doctrine that the laws of nature plus the past necessitate the future: given the laws and the past, only one future is possible. Is determinism compatible with the view that we often act freely? Is determinism compatible with the view that we often are morally responsible for what we do? Is determinism true? Are we free and responsible?

Required Text(s)
Van Inwagen, An Essay on Free Will

Brueckner

Philosophy 152/252G
|
 Plato
|
 ARTS 1251 (TR 9:30-10:45am)
Plato's early dialogues and the Republic. Topics will include methodological issues such as Socratic definition and the Socratic method; the moral views of the Platonic Socrates, including Socratic paradoxes, Socratic intellectualism, and Socrates' objections to traditional rhetoric and sophistry; and Plato's metaphysics and epistemology as developed in the middle books of the Republic . Philosophy 160/260G
|
 Descartes
|
 ARTS 1251 (TR 2:00-3:15pm)
It has been said that much of the 20th century philosophy can be seen as an attempt to break the hold of Descartes on our philosophical sensibilities. Whatever the truth of that, there can be no doubt of Descartes’ enormous influence on the agenda of the past century—e.g., the centrality of epistemology to philosophy, the obsession with skepticism, the mind/body problem (or problems), the nature of the self. Perhaps no other past philosopher except Hume is still so much with us. In this course we will examine Descartes’ conception of the task of philosophy as well as his epistemology, metaphysics and philosophy of mind. We will also look at some illustrative, and more contemporary, alternatives to the Cartesianism.

Required Text(s)
Descartes, Philosophical Writings of Descartes, Volume I
Descartes, Philosophical Writings of Descartes, Volume II
Instructor:
Forgie

Philosophy 165/265G
|
 Hume
|
 PHELPS 1445 (MW 3:00-4:15pm)
This course will examine the philosophy of David Hume (1711-76), addressing his epistemology, metaphysics, ethics, and philosophy of religion. We will focus primarily on Hume’s later philosophical works, the Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding, the Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals and the Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion. We will aim first to understand his theories, second to track down their weaknesses, and third to examine how some of his ideas played out (and continue to play out) in later philosophical epochs.

Required Text(s)
Hume, An Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals
Hume, An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding
Hume, Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion
Instructor:
Holden
