Philosophy 296C: Seminar in the Philosophy of Language

Location & Time

  • Location: Girvetz Hall, Room 2127
  • Time: Wednesdays 12:15–3:00 PM

Description

According to philosophical mythology, the word ‘meter’ (as in ‘3½ meters long’) was introduced as a term for (“defined as”) the length at time t0 of a particular stick S, the so-called standard meter. What then is the length at t0 of S in meters? And how do we know this? Wittgenstein infamously asserted that it cannot be said that S is one meter long, and it also cannot be said that it is not. Kripke disagreed, holding (also infamously) that the statement ‘S is exactly one meter long at t0’ expresses a contingent fact that is nonetheless knowable a priori, at least by the person who so introduced/defined the term. To know the length of a particular stick is to know how long the stick is. Can it really be that one can know a priori how long a particular stick is? This is currently planned as a seminar on controversies concerning the standard meter and especially related issues involving the concept of knowing who and related concepts like those of knowing how long, knowing how many, etc. Among other things, we will go carefully through a currently forthcoming article of Kripke’s on these topics.

Instructor(s)

  • Nathan Salmon

Prerequisite(s)

Graduate standing.

Philosophy 296D: Seminar in the Philosophy of Mind

Location & Time

  • Location: Girvetz Hall, Room 1106
  • Time: Mondays 4:00–6:50 PM

Description

We will discuss psychological explanation, with special emphasis on explanations that cite representational properties of mental states. To what extent does folk psychology already provide compelling psychological explanations? To what extent can representational discourse be transmuted into properly scientific explanations of mental and behavioral outcomes? Should mature scientific psychology jettison representational descriptions and focus instead on neurophysiological descriptions of the brain? We will be particularly concerned to explore how these questions relate to more general issues in the philosophy of science. We will therefore relate the questions to traditional and contemporary theories of scientific explanation, including the deductive-nomological model, interventionism, and abstractionism. We will discuss both armchair philosophical arguments and empirical case studies drawn from scientific psychology. Readings by Davidson, Fodor, Stich, Burge, Block, Lewis, Chalmers, Kim, Woodward, Strevens, Yablo, Putnam, Stalnaker, Williamson, and others.

Instructor(s)

  • Michael Rescorla

Prerequisite(s)

Graduate standing.
